Research

Our work focuses on programmable piezoelectric metamaterials — how electrical circuits connected to periodic structures can reshape the way elastic waves propagate.

Figure: rainbow trapping experiment
01 ── Wave Control
Programmable rainbow trapping and band-gap engineering

In a uniform metamaterial, all unit cells are identical and the band gap is fixed. By gradually varying the resonant frequency of each unit cell's shunt circuit along the beam, we create a graded metamaterial where waves of different frequencies slow down and stop at different spatial positions.

This effect — known as rainbow trapping in analogy with electromagnetics — allows precise spatial control over which frequencies are attenuated where. Using synthetic impedance circuits, the grading profile is entirely digital and can be changed without modifying any hardware.

02 ── Sensing
Spatially programmable wave compression and signal enhancement

When a wave's wavelength is compressed gradually along a waveguide, its energy concentrates — analogous to an acoustic black hole. We implement this using a piezoelectric metamaterial with a numerically optimized refractive index profile, loaded directly from a computer interface.

The result is a reconfigurable wave device that can amplify piezoelectric voltage output at a user-specified frequency and position — a step toward programmable elastic wave sensors that need no mechanical redesign between operating conditions.

03 ── Nonreciprocity
Digitally programmed reciprocity breaking

Reciprocity is a fundamental property of linear time-invariant media — waves propagate identically in both directions. Breaking it requires either nonlinearity, moving parts, or time-varying properties.

We use the third approach: by modulating the inductance of each shunt circuit in both space and time, we create a traveling-wave modulation that breaks the symmetry of the dispersion relation. The result is a directional band gap — a frequency range blocked in one direction but open in the other.
